Fundamental Operations in Algebra


Fundamental operations in algebra are basic mathematical operations used to manipulate algebraic expressions and solve equations. There are four fundamental operations in algebra: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. These operations are the building blocks of algebraic computations and are essential for understanding and solving equations and expressions.

Addition

Addition is the operation of combining two or more numbers or algebraic terms to obtain their sum. In algebra, when adding like terms (terms with the same variable and exponent), we add the coefficients while keeping the variable and exponent unchanged. For example, to add 2x and 3x, we combine the coefficients to get 5x. When adding terms with different variables or exponents, we cannot simplify further.

Subtraction

Subtraction is the operation of taking away one number or algebraic term from another. In algebra, subtraction can be thought of as adding the opposite or negation of a number. When subtracting algebraic terms, we apply the distributive property and combine like terms if possible.

Multiplication

Multiplication is the operation of repeated addition or scaling of a number or algebraic term. In algebra, when multiplying algebraic terms, we apply the distributive property and multiply coefficients and combine variables using the laws of exponents. When multiplying constants or like terms, we simply multiply their coefficients.

Division

Division is the operation of splitting a number or algebraic expression into equal parts. In algebra, division involves finding the quotient by dividing one term by another. Division by zero is undefined in algebra.

These four fundamental operations can be combined and used iteratively to solve more complex equations and expressions. It is crucial to follow the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS) while performing these operations to ensure accurate results. Parentheses, exponents, multiplication, and division should be performed before addition and subtraction.

Exponents

Exponents in algebra are a way to represent repeated multiplication of a number or a variable by itself. They are also referred to as powers. An exponent is a small number written above and to the right of a base number or variable. The exponent indicates how many times the base is multiplied by itself.

When the exponent is a positive integer, it represents repeated multiplication. For example, 2^3 means 2 multiplied by itself three times: 2 * 2 * 2, resulting in 8.
